**Title:** Screen Capture Rectification and Temporal Stabilization for Real-world Captured-screen Videos

**Description:** Screen demoiréing and screen-image restoration studies show that captured-screen content is a meaningful image processing problem. However, many datasets start after an important step has already been solved: the screen region is cropped, aligned, or paired with a clean reference. Real phone-captured videos are less controlled. They contain background regions, perspective distortion, hand-held camera shake, weak screen borders, glare, moiré patterns, and dynamic screen content. This project studies that missing geometric preprocessing step, converting full captured-screen videos into rectified and temporally stable screen-coordinate videos for later restoration, OCR, or archival use.

**Task and goal:** Given a hand-held video of a computer screen, the task is to estimate the screen plane over time and render the screen content as a front-facing video. The output should suppress non-screen background, perspective distortion, and frame-to-frame jitter while preserving the physical aspect ratio of the screen. This makes the video easier to inspect and more suitable for later restoration tasks.

**Method:** The method follows the distinction between screen motion and content motion. It first obtains the screen quadrilateral by automatic detection or manual annotation, then estimates the main homography from the physical screen boundary. Edge filtering and LSD/Hough line detection are used to locate the four borders, while inner Lucas-Kanade feature points serve only as a consistency check. If inner motion conflicts with border motion under RANSAC, it is treated as screen-content motion and excluded from homography estimation. Low border confidence, low inlier ratio, or invalid quadrilateral shape triggers border re-detection; if recovery fails, the system freezes the last valid homography and marks invalid regions. The final video is rectified to the estimated native screen aspect ratio, centered on the target canvas, and smoothed over time to suppress high-frequency jitter.

**Dataset and experiment:** The evaluation will use a self-collected real-video set with 5 scenario classes, 10 clips per class, and about 5 seconds per clip. The scenarios are chosen to cover both ordinary usage and difficult cases, including static pages, scrolling pages, in-screen video playback, PPT or weak-border pages, and frames affected by glare, moiré patterns, or partial screen loss. To connect visual comparison with quantitative evaluation, selected key frames from each class will be manually annotated with four screen corners.

**Evaluation metrics:** The metrics follow the requirements of the task. Geometry is evaluated on annotated key frames by corner error, quadrilateral IoU, and aspect-ratio error, which directly measure whether the screen is located and rectified correctly. Temporal stability is evaluated by residual adjacent-frame translation, rotation, and scale variation in the normalized video. Since rectification can blur high-frequency detail, texture-rich patches will also be compared before and after rectification using average gradient magnitude and an edge preservation index at the same screen-coordinate scale. For moiré or regular-grid hard cases, 2D FFT is used to check whether dominant horizontal and vertical frequency directions become closer to an orthogonal screen grid. These measures connect geometric correctness, video stability, and signal preservation without requiring a downstream demoiréing model.

**Expected results:** The expected outcome is a preprocessing pipeline based on classical image processing and geometric vision. It will convert real captured-screen videos into stable, front-facing screen videos while preserving the screen shape and aspect ratio. The final report will compare frame-wise detection, content-based optical-flow tracking, and the proposed border-guided tracking strategy, explaining when each method works and where it fails.
