**Title:** Screen Capture Rectification and Temporal Stabilization for Real-world Captured-screen Videos

**Description:** Existing screen demoiréing and screen-image restoration studies show that captured-screen content is a meaningful image processing problem. However, many public datasets are collected in controlled settings, where the screen region is already cropped, aligned, or paired with a clean reference. In real phone-captured videos, the input usually contains background regions, perspective distortion, hand-held camera shake, weak or missing screen borders, moiré patterns, glare, and dynamic screen content. This project focuses on this missing preprocessing stage: converting a full captured-screen video into a rectified and temporally stable screen-coordinate video before downstream demoiréing, OCR, or archival restoration.

**Task and goal:** The input is a hand-held video of a computer screen. The output is a front-facing and temporally stable video of the screen content. The goal is to remove most non-screen background, correct perspective distortion, reduce frame-to-frame jitter, preserve the physical aspect ratio of the screen, and provide a reliable normalized input for later restoration tasks.

**Method:** The proposed pipeline first obtains the screen quadrilateral by automatic detection or manual annotation. The main homography is estimated from the physical screen boundary, not from moving inner content: edge filtering and LSD/Hough line detection are used to track the four screen borders, while inner Lucas-Kanade feature points are used only as a consistency check. If inner motion conflicts with border motion under RANSAC, it is treated as screen-content motion and ignored for homography estimation. When the border confidence is low, the inlier ratio falls below a threshold, or the quadrilateral becomes invalid, the system reinitializes border detection; if reinitialization fails, it freezes the last valid homography and marks invalid regions. The output is rectified to the estimated native screen aspect ratio and then centered on the target canvas, and corner trajectories are smoothed to suppress high-frequency jitter.

**Dataset and experiment:** The evaluation will use a self-collected real-video set with 5 scenario classes, 10 clips per class, and about 5 seconds per clip: static webpages/documents, scrolling webpages, in-screen video playback, PPT or weak-border low-texture pages, and hard cases with glare, moiré patterns, or partial screen loss. Several key frames from each class will be manually annotated with four screen corners to provide geometric reference labels.

**Evaluation metrics:** The evaluation is organized into four levels. At the geometry level, manually annotated key frames will be used to measure whether the recovered screen quadrilateral is correct, using corner error, quadrilateral IoU, and aspect-ratio error. At the temporal level, the normalized videos will be checked for residual jitter through adjacent-frame translation, rotation, and scale variation. At the signal-preservation level, texture-rich patches will be compared before and after rectification using average gradient magnitude and an edge preservation index, after mapping both patches to the same screen-coordinate scale. At the spectral level, moiré or regular-grid hard cases will be analyzed with 2D FFT to measure whether the dominant horizontal and vertical frequency directions become closer to an orthogonal screen grid. Failure cases will also be grouped by cause, such as weak borders, dynamic content, glare, and partial screen loss.

**Expected results:** The expected result is a classical image-processing and geometric-vision pipeline that produces stable and geometrically correct screen videos from realistic captured-screen inputs. The final report will compare frame-wise detection, content-based optical-flow tracking, and the proposed border-guided tracking strategy; summarize successful and failed cases; and discuss when this preprocessing is sufficient for downstream screen video restoration.
